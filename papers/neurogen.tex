\begin{abstract}
We investigate whether biologically-inspired developmental principles can improve transformer training. Across 200+ controlled experiments at two scales (3.4M and 125M parameters), we systematically test cellular automata weight initialization, live developmental rules, embryogenic development, pre-wired circuits, and architectural constraints inspired by cortical development. Most approaches produce marginal gains (+0.6\%) or actively hurt ($-3$\% to $-5$\%). However, embedding a developmental constraint into the architecture---attention windows that grow from local to global across layers---yields robust improvements that increase with scale: $+1.5$\% at 3.4M parameters ($p=0.001$, Cohen's $d=2.05$, 5 seeds) and up to $+12.9$\% at 125M parameters (50k steps, gap still widening). Three targeted mechanism experiments eliminate gradient noise removal, softmax coupling contamination, and variance reduction as explanations, establishing that the benefit is specific to attention restriction rather than a generic gradient quality effect. Several hypotheses remain consistent with the data---forced architectural specialization, implicit regularization, optimization landscape effects, inductive bias, and curriculum learning---and await further investigation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Standard transformers start from random initialization with uniform, fully-global attention at every layer. Gradient descent discovers all internal structure---specialized attention heads, hierarchical layer roles, induction circuits---from scratch.

The brain takes a different approach. Genetic developmental programs grow functional structure before learning begins: cortical receptive fields develop local-to-global through layers, induction-like circuits (pattern completion) are pre-wired, and architectural constraints (laminar structure, lateral inhibition, critical periods) protect these scaffolds during early learning.

We ask: can these developmental principles improve transformer training?

Our answer is nuanced. Most biological inspirations fail when applied to transformers. But one specific intervention works substantially: constraining attention to grow from local to global across layers, mimicking cortical receptive field development. When combined with pre-wired induction head circuits, the improvement reaches $+5.2$\% at 3.4M parameters. At 125M parameters, the quartic window schedule alone produces $+12.9$\% improvement over baseline after 50k training steps, with the gap still widening.

The key insight is that the mechanism operates through \textit{forced architectural specialization}, not gradient noise removal. Three targeted experiments demonstrate that attention windows increase gradient signal coherence $18\times$ without reducing gradient noise, that softmax coupling contributes only 4--7\% gradient contamination, and that larger batch sizes (which reduce gradient variance) cannot replicate the window effect at equal token count.

\section{Related Work}

\textbf{Neural Cellular Automata.} Growing Neural Cellular Automata \citep{mordvintsev2020growing} demonstrated self-organizing pattern generation. HyperNCA \citep{najarro2022hypernca} extended this to grow RL policy weights via NCA. Our work tests NCA-inspired approaches for transformer weight initialization and live training dynamics.

\textbf{Structured Initialization.} Mimetic initialization \citep{trockman2023mimetic} analytically constructs attention patterns ($QK^\top \approx I$). T-Fixup \citep{huang2020tfixup} uses Lipschitz-constrained initialization. $\mu$P \citep{yang2022mup} provides principled parameterization for scaling. Our approach uses architectural constraints rather than analytical weight formulas.

\textbf{Universal Circuits.} Induction heads \citep{olsson2022incontext} are universal match-and-copy circuits found across trained transformers. Circuit tracing \citep{anthropic2025circuits} reveals consistent internal structure. Our work attempts to pre-build these circuits and finds they require architectural protection to survive early training.

\textbf{Efficient Attention.} Sliding window attention \citep{beltagy2020longformer} reduces computation for long sequences. Flash Attention \citep{dao2023flashattention2} provides hardware-efficient attention with native sliding window support. Our contribution is framing layer-wise window growth as a \textit{developmental} constraint that improves training quality, not just efficiency.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Experimental Framework}

We follow the autoresearch paradigm \citep{karpathy2026autoresearch}: an autonomous agent modifies training code, runs fixed-budget experiments, keeps improvements, and discards failures. Over 200 experiments were conducted across two model scales.

\textbf{3.4M model:} GPT-style transformer, depth 4, channels 256, 4 heads. Training data: TinyStories \citep{eldan2023tinystories}. Hardware: Apple M1 Pro (MPS). Metric: validation bits-per-byte (val\_bpb).

\textbf{125M model:} GPT-2 Small architecture (12 layers, 768 dim, 12 heads, 1024 context). Training data: FineWeb-Edu \citep{penedo2024fineweb}. Hardware: NVIDIA H100 80GB. Uses RoPE, RMSNorm, bfloat16 mixed precision, and Flash Attention 2 with native sliding window support.

\subsection{Developmental Attention Windows}

Each layer $l$ (of $L$ total) has a maximum attention window determined by a power function:
\begin{equation}
    w(l) = \min\!\Big(T,\; b + \Big(\frac{l+1}{L}\Big)^{\!\gamma} (T - b)\Big)
    \label{eq:window}
\end{equation}
where $T$ is the sequence length, $b$ is the base window size (8 for 3.4M, 16 for 125M), and $\gamma$ is the growth exponent. Position $i$ can attend to positions $[\max(0, i - w(l) + 1),\, i]$.

For $\gamma = 4$ (quartic) at depth 4, the window schedule is $[8, 10, 65, 256]$---early layers see only local context while the final layer has full attention. We tested exponents from 0.5 to 12.0 along with sigmoid, logarithmic, exponential, and Fibonacci schedules. Quartic ($\gamma = 3$--$4$) consistently performs best.

\textbf{Implementation:} The window is applied as a causal attention mask. At 3.4M scale, this uses standard masked attention. At 125M scale, we use Flash Attention 2's native \texttt{window\_size} parameter, which provides sliding window attention within the fused kernel---windowed models run \textit{faster} than baseline (2.84 vs 2.78 steps/sec).

\subsection{Approaches Tested}

We organized 26 approaches into four categories:

\textbf{Category A: Weight Decoration} (modify weights, preserve architecture). CA initialization (grid, block-diagonal, reaction-diffusion, spectral); live CA during training (homeostatic, competition, pruning); embryogenic CA (activity-dependent rules using gradients during critical period); pre-wired circuits (induction heads, layer-role differentiation).

\textbf{Category B: Architectural Constraints} (change how the model computes). Attention window growth schedules (linear, quadratic, quartic, higher powers); CA-generated attention bias.

\textbf{Category C: Combinations.} Windows + induction pre-wiring; windows + embryogenic CA; all combined.

\textbf{Category D: Radical modifications.} CA modulation channels; token vitality (cell death); sleep consolidation; cross-layer CA state; developmental dropout.

\section{Mechanism Analysis}

We conducted three experiments to identify why attention windows improve training.

\subsection{Experiment 1: Gradient Quality vs Window Size}

On a frozen trained 3.4M checkpoint, we measured gradient properties at 10 window sizes (8--256 tokens, 50 backward passes each, layer 0 only).

\textbf{Key finding:} Gradient noise norm is \textit{constant} ($\sim$0.0053) across all window sizes. What changes is signal norm---it increases $18\times$ from window 256 (0.0017) to window 8 (0.032). The gradient signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) shows a knee at $\sim$32--48 tokens, matching the model's natural attention span (Table~\ref{tab:gradient}).

\subsection{Mechanism Conclusion}

\subsection{Eliminated Hypotheses}

Three mechanism hypotheses are ruled out by the experimental evidence:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Gradient noise removal.} If windows clean gradients by removing contributions from non-attended positions, noise norm should decrease with smaller windows. It does not---noise norm is constant at $\sim$0.0053 across all window sizes (Experiment~1).
    \item \textbf{Softmax coupling contamination.} If the softmax backward pass introduces significant gradient contamination through position coupling, the noise fraction from non-attended positions should be large ($>30$\%). It is only 4--7\% (Experiment~2).
    \item \textbf{Variance reduction.} If windows improve training by reducing gradient variance (fewer sources $=$ less variance), larger batch sizes should replicate the effect. At equal token count, larger batch is \textit{worse} than baseline, while windows improve it (Experiment~3).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Surviving Hypotheses}

Several explanations remain consistent with all observed data. Distinguishing between them requires experiments we have not run.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Forced architectural specialization.} Early layers constrained to local attention must build compositional features (n-gram patterns, syntactic boundaries) that later layers leverage for global integration. The increased gradient signal coherence observed in Experiment~1 would be a consequence of this specialization, not a cause.
    \item \textbf{Implicit regularization.} Window constraints reduce the effective hypothesis space of each layer, acting as a form of structural regularization that prevents overfitting to spurious long-range correlations early in training.
    \item \textbf{Optimization landscape effect.} Partitioning attention by depth may create a smoother or more favorable loss landscape, allowing gradient descent to find qualitatively different (and better) local minima.
    \item \textbf{Inductive bias toward compositionality.} The local-to-global structure may encode a useful prior about natural language hierarchy that happens to align with the structure of the training data.
    \item \textbf{Curriculum effect.} The layer-wise window schedule may function as an implicit curriculum where the model learns local patterns before global ones, analogous to curriculum learning in training schedules.
\end{itemize}

These hypotheses are not mutually exclusive---several may contribute simultaneously. The key empirical finding is that the mechanism is \textit{specific to attention restriction}: it cannot be replicated by generic gradient quality improvements (larger batch, gradient filtering) and operates through a pathway distinct from noise removal.

\section{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item 125M results are preliminary (2 seeds at 50k steps, models not converged).
    \item No downstream task evaluation (CORE, MMLU, etc.).
    \item The 3.4M model uses TinyStories (narrow domain); 125M uses FineWeb-Edu (broader).
    \item Optimal exponent at depth 12 not yet determined (tested only quartic).
    \item Mechanism experiments performed only at 3.4M scale.
    \item The surviving mechanism hypotheses have not been experimentally distinguished.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}

\textbf{Scaling.} The effect grows with scale ($+1.5$\% at 3.4M $\rightarrow$ $+12.9$\% at 125M), and the gap is still widening at 50k steps. More layers provide a richer developmental progression, and the models are far from convergence.

\textbf{Practical implications.} Attention windows require zero additional parameters. With Flash Attention's native sliding window support, windowed models run \textit{faster} than baseline. This is a free training improvement with a throughput bonus.

\textbf{Biological connection.} The optimal configuration mirrors cortical development: extremely local receptive fields at early processing stages, with global integration only at the highest levels. The quartic growth exponent creates a steep curve---most layers stay very local, with only the final layers opening to full context.

\textbf{Mechanism.} We have strong evidence for what the mechanism is \textit{not} (gradient noise removal, softmax coupling, variance reduction) but multiple remaining candidates for what it \textit{is}. Distinguishing forced specialization from implicit regularization, optimization landscape effects, inductive bias, and curriculum effects would require targeted ablations---for example, randomizing the layer-to-window assignment (which preserves total compute but breaks the local-to-global hierarchy) or applying windows only during early training then removing them (which tests whether the benefit is from permanent constraint or early-training curriculum).

\section{Conclusion}

We systematically tested 26 biologically-inspired approaches across 200+ experiments at two scales. Most fail. Developmental attention window growth---constraining early layers to local attention with quartic growth toward full attention---produces consistent improvements: $+1.5$\% at 3.4M parameters ($p=0.001$, 5 seeds) and $+12.9$\% at 125M parameters (50k steps, gap widening). Three mechanism experiments eliminate gradient noise removal, softmax coupling contamination, and variance reduction as explanations, establishing that the benefit is specific to attention restriction rather than a generic gradient quality effect. Several hypotheses---forced specialization, implicit regularization, optimization landscape effects, inductive bias, and curriculum learning---remain consistent with the data and await further investigation. The approach requires no additional parameters, no extra compute, and produces faster models when combined with Flash Attention's sliding window support.
